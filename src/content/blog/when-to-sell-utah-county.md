Ask ten people when to sell a house and nine will say spring. They're not wrong — they're just answering a different question than the one that matters. The real question isn't "when do the most buyers show up?" It's "when do *you* net the most from *your* house?" Those are not the same thing.

## Why spring works — and why that's the problem

Spring brings the most buyers. It also brings the most sellers. More competition means your house is one open house among six on the same Saturday, and buyers comparison-shop you in real time. High season lifts prices, but it lifts everyone's — your *relative* advantage is zero.

## When off-season selling wins

A well-priced house in November has something a June listing never gets: buyers with no alternatives and real urgency. Corporate relocations, lease expirations, life changes — these buyers don't wait for tulips. Fewer showings, but every one of them is serious. I've seen off-season sellers net cleaner offers precisely because the tire-kickers stayed home.

## The three questions that actually decide your timing

1. **Where are you going?** If you're buying your next home in the same market, the season mostly cancels out — you sell high and buy high, or sell low and buy low. Timing games only pay when you're changing markets or renting between homes.
2. **Is the house ready?** A rushed spring listing loses to a prepared winter one. Deferred repairs, bad photos, and wishful pricing cost more than any calendar effect. (The framing contractor in me begs you: fix the squeaks before the photographer comes.)
3. **What does your street say?** Neighborhood-level supply beats seasonal wisdom every time. If three comparable homes are sitting unsold on your street, it's not your season — it's your price.

## The honest summary

Spring is a real tailwind, worth maybe a few points of convenience. Your pricing, preparation, and personal situation are worth far more. Sell when your life says sell — and let a data-driven price, not the calendar, do the heavy lifting.
