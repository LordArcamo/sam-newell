I bought my first property at the bottom of the recession, in 2010, as a broke construction management student at BYU. Today, through Multifamily Investment Club, we own and manage more than 2,000 doors. Here's what the road between those two sentences actually taught me — especially the parts the passive-income influencers skip.

## "Passive" is a spectrum, not a switch

Nothing real pays you for zero effort. What multifamily offers is *front-loaded* effort: the work concentrates in underwriting, acquisition, and building the right operating team. Do those three ruthlessly well, and the monthly involvement genuinely shrinks. Do them casually, and you've bought yourself a second job with tenants.

## Buy the problem, not the polish

Every deal we've been proudest of looked mediocre in the listing photos. Value-add means the current owner's neglect is your margin — under-market rents, tired units, sloppy operations. The polished, stabilized building is priced for perfection; the fixable problem is priced for you.

## Cash flow forgives, appreciation rewards

We hold long-term, and the reason is simple: cash flow keeps you alive through every market, and appreciation pays you for having survived. Investors who need to sell in a down year lose. Investors who can *wait* in a down year get to buy from them. I started my career buying from people who couldn't wait — 2010 taught me which side of that trade to be on.

## The spreadsheet is the easy part

Underwriting is teachable — the CCIM curriculum will happily teach it to you. What's harder is the discipline to walk away when the numbers say walk, even after you've toured the property, met the broker, and started imagining the win. Every bad deal I've ever seen was a good spreadsheet that got emotionally renegotiated.

## Your first door is the expensive education

Nobody's first deal is their best deal. It's tuition. The goal of door number one isn't returns — it's learning the machine: leases, maintenance calls, taxes, refinancing. Keep it small enough that the lessons are affordable.
